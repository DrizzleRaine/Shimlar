How to make a roleplay character for Shimlar

 

Character History

 

Even the most unreal fantasy is be based in part on the way our real world functions. Physical laws, the extent of magic's power, etc can be manipulated, but some things cannot. You have had experiences before this moment and so has your character if it is to be believable. Think of this, try to make the background fit the character or vice versa. It's unlikely a child in a loving family, well-educated and adored, would grow up to be evil.

 

There's no need to be complicated and detailed. Anything from a list to a fleshed out story will work. A list such as the following will do, but for the long term, a story may help you keep in touch with your character's most important traits and behaviors.

 

Example of listing:

1. Character was born a duchess.

2. Her personality was always mischievous and perky.

3. She had one brother, who treated her like spun glass.

4. When she was sixteen, she fell in love. He didn't return her feelings and her heart was broken. This turned her bitter toward men for a while.

5. When she was seventeen, she was sent on a tour of Europe with her best friend chaperoning. Her friend was killed in a robbery while they were out and about and she began to lust for revenge.

6. Sent a letter home soon after from a different identity, stating that there had been two bodies found, instead of one.

7. She began doing what she does now, hunting down any who shared the profession which caused her friend to die and taking her revenge on them.

 

Character abilities

 

Have you ever met someone who wielded a blade from infancy? No one has and your character is no different. Was he trained by his father? Perhaps it was forbidden to his social class and no opportunity came to learn on his own. Training can be the most important feature of character history, since it affects nearly everything they become. Perspective, body shape, religion, health can all stem from training or lack of it. But keep in mind, gods are not made in a few years. Creating a god character for roleplay can be very easy and is certainly tempting, but gods are usually dull to play and losing every battle tends to make other roleplayers very unhappy with you. A well-made character has flaws, weaknesses, which make them more interesting and easier to relate to.

 

Remember that magical items would be very expensive in a fantasy world, as it is difficult to enchant things of the material planes. And uniquely created magical equipment (exotic weaponry, strange materials, etc.) is almost unheard of. You say your character was the son of a maharajah who liked to steal sacred artifacts? Then he just may have benefited by swiping a djinn's prison lamp. But the social class capable of collecting such items makes up a very small percent of the population. And again, it comes to interest. Your character will be more realistic and more appealing if it has to struggle sometimes.

 

All this is not to say a powerful character can't be played. Some have been transferred from other games, tabletop roleplaying, or another story. Still, you, the writer, must have a good concept of its history and power. New character or used, you should be able to explain how he learned that disastrous Sol's Shrieking Wave and what it does.

 

Examples:

 

Stricken Shield - Active enchantment
Veils of crackling electricity cling to the caster. Magic reduction: Lightning %50, Cold %10. Melee: chance to stun opponent.

Wreckage - Wind Spell
Raises all loose matter within a circle 200 ft in diameter.

Blood Flame - Psionic/Transmutation
Developed from the Blighters' "Combust Vitality" feat. Always active, this ability transforms fires into harmless, if useless, "dead blood", giving the vampiric user no health or psychic benefits as would live feeding.

 

Description...description...description...

 

What is it and why do it?

 

The better you describe what you are doing, the better a reader's or roleplaying partner's understanding will be. It makes you able to exert your character's influence with taking from another's (as in autoing and godmoding). When describing, keep in mind your character's body, mind, and environment.

 

Body: A sprite will not have the physical strength to melee with a flame demon. It may, however, prove faster than the beast and use its quickness and agility to make the demon trip over itself or collide with the pillars of its lair.

 

Mind: Your character is facing the trial of its life up to this point: a series of rooms with lethal traps in abundance. Why? She's found herself in subsurface monastery and the only way to pass is to prove she has the discipline and resourcefulness needed to be honored by the monks' leader. Well, does she? Does her ability to think rationally tend to break down when she's desperate? Does she become frustrated, furious or emboldened by trial and error?

 

Environment: If a winged creature leaps from a thatched roof and pumps its wings to begin flying, the downdraft will stir the straw in the roof, there will be a sound of air moving through it and perhaps even a smell, sweet or molded.

 

An instance of detail.

 

Alan Twinwalker walks down the road toward the tavern.

 

VS.

 

Alan Twinwalker walks down the gravel road, toward the tall, glistening building that was the tavern, his footsteps padding slightly across the ground, sheathed blade thudding quietly against his thigh.

 

Which gave you a better picture of Alan and what he was doing? The second, of course.

 

Describe to make known what is happening, how, and why.

 

Terms roleplayers use

 

The /e command

In Shimlar, it's generally best to use the emote command for roleplaying. For one thing, it is easier on the eyes. And it is less disruptive to other people in the room, who are most likely using the same command.

 

Example: /e wants to play. On your screen, after striking "Enter" or clicking "Send", your post will appear with your name in white, followed by the text in tan/gold. Yourname wants to play.

 

RP = roleplay

 

-c-, ~c~, /c/, ... = continued

Shimlar's max post length is about two lines. Frequently, a post can't be confined to this limit, and there's no reason to hold yourself to it. There are many styles of the -c- abbreviation, but they all mean the writer has not finished.

 

Unless your character means to deliberately interrupt the other, please let the writer complete their post.

 

NPC = non-player character

An NPC is a character a player controls that is not the character you normally write about. They can be any necessary being, but are usually found as servants, students/teachers, peasants, innkeepers etc.

 

OOC = Out of character

Talking as yourself, not your character. In Shimlar, most often indicated as /e ((OOCmessagehere)).

 

IC = In character

Self-explanatory and the bulk of roleplaying. Anything your character does, not you.

 

AFK = away from keyboard

 

BRB = be right back

 

GTG = got to go

 

Bookmark/pause

Since roleplaying can consume a lot of time, whether soloing or or with other players, you may occasionally need to pause events and resume them some other time.

 

:: :: = NPC actions

These are often used to seperate the NPC from the player character.

 

Mun/Mundane

You, the player, the writer, who controls your character.

 

Tips for beginners

 

Haven't roleplayed in text before? Or perhaps not at all? Here are some ideas to polish your writing and make your roleplaying more enjoyable for you and the reader.

 

1. Take your time. Bookmark wherever you need to. The beauty of written RP is that you don't have to counter that attack within seconds of real time. Fantasy moves at your pace.

 

2. Remember how to -play-. Tolkien: great storyteller, dry author. To keep things moving, you can't get bogged down in explanation and description. Even in the dullest moments, your character is doing something, active in some way. At slow times like this, build anticipation of action. Play. Make his expression reveal his thoughts, have onlookers react. What is affected by your character keeps him alive.

 

3. Experimentation and mistakes. Hey, it's a game. You don't have to be a pro. You're welcome to learn everything the hard way if you like and some of us have to. So try something, anything, the rules are loose. Your plan for your character requires the involvement of half a dozen powerful NPCs putting him on trial? You want to test a new spell, or entirely new method of magic, which your character longs to develop? Try it out. Even if you don't make it work so well, practice will pinpoint the problems for you. And there's no reason you can't redo it.

 

4. Read. You don't have to be a pro. But professional fiction can be an inspiration, an exercise for your imagination, and a guide for technique. Watch the roleplayers you think are very good, ask them if they've had inspiration from an author, then read from the suggestion. A good author for study will be able to make even a bizarre character feel natural, impose on your thoughts and emotions, and keep you reading even through the slow points.
